Two-panel slide. Each panel is a chart with a one-line takeaway.

- Instagram: 2.5 years

Takeaway under the chart: *Nothing in consumer tech history has scaled this fast.*

**RIGHT panel — Anthropic / Claude Code ARR (growth line)**
Data points to plot:

Takeaway under the chart: *AI products are crossing $1B faster than any SaaS category in history. The curve is still bending up.*

**Speaker note:** This is the "why you should care" slide. Don't over-explain — let the visuals do it. Pause for 5 seconds after each chart. The point is *velocity*, not the specific numbers. Two reactions you want: (1) "this isn't just hype" and (2) "if it's growing that fast, my job will look different in 12 months."

### Slide 4 — The macro frame (4-card)

Small label: **THE MACRO FRAME**
Title: *Four things are shifting at the same time.*

- **01 — Models stopped being the bottleneck.**
  The frontier matters less than the workflow. Companies compete on *who put it to work*, not which model they picked.
- **02 — Agents are leaving the demo phase.**
  Gartner expects 40% of enterprise apps to ship with task-specific AI agents by end of 2026. They schedule, draft, route, follow up.
- **03 — Output is commoditizing.**
  Drafts, summaries, decks, screens, first-pass code — all near-free. Whatever used to be "the deliverable" no longer is.
- **04 — The bar moved. Quietly.**
  The floor went up (anyone can produce something passable). The ceiling went up more. The gap between *good* and *great* is now fully visible.

**Speaker note:** Pause on point 4 — that's the one HR feels first in calibrations and performance.

### Slide 5 — One stat that should land
Big number: **~80%**
Subline: of enterprise workplace apps will have AI copilots embedded by end of 2026 *(IDC)*.

### Slide 6 — What changed in the last 12 months (timeline strip)
Small label: **MOMENTUM**
Five beats on a horizontal timeline:
- Reasoning models → real planning, not just answers
- Multimodal default → AI sees screenshots, files, charts
- Memory & context → agents that remember across days
- Action, not advice → AI does the click, the send, the update
- Cost collapse → inference ~10× cheaper than 18 months ago

**Speaker note:** Don't read all five. Pick two and tell a 30-sec story for each.

### Slide 7 — What this isn't
Two-column "What's actually happening / What's not":

| What's actually happening | What's *not* happening |
|---|---|

| Some entry-level work compressing | Senior judgment becoming obsolete |
| Routine tasks automated | Human relationships automated |
| New roles appearing (30+ new titles in HR/IT alone) | A clean before/after moment |

**Speaker note:** This slide buys you trust. Naming the hype lets you talk seriously about the real shifts.

# SECTION 2 — PEOPLE, TEAMS, AND HR (14 min) ⭐ HEART OF THE TALK

### Slide 9 — Two conversations at once
Small label: **HOW TO LISTEN TO THIS SECTION**
Title: *You're sitting in two seats today.*

- **Seat 1 — Your work as HR.** Recruiting, BP work, ops, comms — all changing.
- **Seat 2 — The org around you.** Engineers, PMs, finance, support — all changing differently.

You can't help the second seat if you haven't done the work in the first.

### Slide 10 — Your work — what shifts (4-card)
Small label: **THE WORK ITSELF**
Title: *What HR does, redrawn.*

- **Recruiting.** Sourcing and screening compress. Interviews stay human. Calibration gets harder because every candidate has an AI co-pilot. → *Spend the freed time on signal, not throughput.*
- **HRBP.** Faster diagnostics (sentiment, churn risk, comp benchmarks in minutes). The conversation with the manager is the value — the prep isn't. → *Show up with a point of view, not a deck.*
- **People Ops.** Forms, FAQs, policy lookups → handled by agents. Cases that escalate to you get harder, not easier. → *Skill up on the edge cases, not the volume.*
- **Office / workplace.** Scheduling, vendor coordination, logistics → agentic. The judgment calls (who needs what, when, why) → still you. → *Curate the experience, don't run the queue.*

**Speaker note:** Be honest: some work goes away. The work that replaces it is more strategic and more exposed. That's good news, but it's not free.

### Slide 11 — Headcount: reshaped, not just reduced ⭐ REFRAMED
Small label: **THE HEADCOUNT QUESTION**
Title: *It's not "fewer people." It's "different people."*

Three cards:
- **Different skill mix.** The roles that compress are the ones AI does well. The roles that grow are the ones that direct AI, design workflows, and own judgment. A team doesn't shrink — its *composition* changes.
- **Shift, not delete.** AI is **5.7×** more likely to *shift* responsibilities than to *eliminate* a role (SHRM, 2026). The dominant pattern is re-bundling, not layoffs.
- **New roles are appearing.** 30+ new job titles inside HR and IT alone in the last 18 months — AI enablement leads, workflow designers, automation owners, productization PMs.

Bottom line, full-width: *Fewer of some seats. More of others. Same total headcount, very different team.*

**Speaker note:** Read the room. Some people in this audience worry headcount is a euphemism for layoffs. Be direct: the *composition* shift is real and uncomfortable, but the headline number is more about reshaping than reducing. The work for HR is to *own* that reshape — not be the team it happens to.

### Slide 12 — Roles are changing — ICs and leaders, differently ⭐ NEW
Small label: **WHAT WE NEED FROM PEOPLE**
Title: *The bar moved for everyone — but it moved differently.*

Two-column layout: **Individual Contributors** vs **Leaders**.

| Individual Contributors | Leaders |
|---|---|
| From *executing tasks* → *directing AI to execute tasks* | From *assigning work* → *designing the workflow AI and humans share* |
| From *deep specialty only* → *T-shaped: depth + adjacent fluency* | From *managing throughput* → *managing taste and standards* |
| From *"what should I do?"* → *"here's what I decided"* | From *reviewing artifacts* → *evaluating judgment calls* |
| From *waiting for clarity* → *operating with ambiguity* | From *team-as-pyramid* → *team-as-pod* |

Bottom strip: **What grows for both — the soft skills.**
Communication · Judgment · Curiosity · Comfort with ambiguity · Standards & taste

**Speaker note:** This is the slide that makes the abstract concrete for HR. Stress: the *same* soft-skills cluster shows up in both columns. Coursera saw +120% YoY in critical-thinking enrollments — the market is already pricing this in. Slide 14 will go deeper on these skills; here just plant the flag.

### Slide 13 — Team shapes are changing (visual: org chart morphing)
Small label: **TEAM SHAPES**
Title: *Smaller teams. Wider scope. More T-shaped people.*

Three observations:
- **The pyramid is flattening.** Fewer entry-level "do the task" roles. More "design the workflow" roles.
- **Specialists are bundling.** A "PM" today is PM + research analyst + light designer + light data analyst. A recruiter is recruiter + market analyst + content creator + employer-brand strategist.
- **Pods over departments.** Cross-functional 3–8 person units are out-shipping 30-person orgs in some companies. (Seed-stage AI startups: median 3–5 engineers shipping product to millions.)

**Speaker note:** Connect to the ways-of-working conversations leadership is already having. This is *why* the squad/pod framing is showing up — it's a response to this shift, not a fad.

### Slide 14 — What gets more valuable (4-card)
Small label: **WHAT DOESN'T GET OUTSOURCED**
Title: *The skills that just appreciated.*

- **Judgment.** Choosing under uncertainty. Knowing when the AI is wrong. (Coursera: +120% YoY in critical-thinking enrollments — not a coincidence.)
- **Communication.** AI writes the draft. *You* still own what's said and how it lands.
- **Relationships.** AI doesn't hear hesitation in a 1:1. It doesn't read the room. Still the job.
- **Taste & standards.** When everyone can produce something passable, the people who know what *good* looks like become the constraint.

**Speaker note:** Don't frame these as "consolation prizes" — frame them as the new top of the market. Gartner says 50% of orgs will require "AI-free" skills assessments by 2026 *because* this stuff matters more.

### Slide 15 — Section close
Big line: *"AI didn't invent these skills."*
Sub: *It removed the excuses for not practicing them.*

### Slide 18 — How the Platform org is changing ⭐ REWRITTEN (absorbs old slide 18)
Small label: **INTERNAL — WHAT'S CHANGING IN PLATFORM**
Title: *Different structure. Different lifecycle. Different muscle.*

Three cards:

- **01 — A new "Core" domain.**
  A dedicated domain for the shared substrate every product team builds on — data, AI, foundational services. The point: stop reinventing, start composing. For HR: this surfaces new role archetypes (platform PMs, infra-leaning engineers, AI workflow owners) that need clear ladders and rubrics.

- **02 — A different development lifecycle.**
  Faster iteration. Prototype-first. AI in every step from design → spec → code → review → deploy. For HR: performance conversations need to reference *AI-leveraged* output, and calibration language needs to catch up. "What did they decide?" replaces "How polished was the artifact?"

- **03 — Education & enablement: Foundry + community.**
  Foundry is the program turning AI capability into shipped impact across functions; the community is how that capability spreads horizontally. For HR: this is your partner. Hiring profiles, onboarding, growth conversations should all point here.

**Speaker note:** This is where the talk gets practical for HR. Don't read each card — pick the one most relevant to the audience and go deep for 60 seconds. The point: Platform isn't a team you support — it's the way the company is starting to operate, and HR has to keep up with the artifacts (JDs, ladders, rubrics) that describe it.

### Slide 19 — What HR specifically needs to be doing now (5-card) ⭐ NEW BULLET
Small label: **YOUR PLAYBOOK**
Title: *Five things to start this quarter.*

- **01 — Re-read every JD.** Half describe a job that's already changed shape. Start with the highest-volume reqs.
- **02 — Recruiters: partner earlier with hiring managers.** Push them on the *right* skills and *soft* skills, not just the role title. Half the calibration problem today is JDs and hiring-manager debriefs that haven't caught up with what they actually need.
- **03 — Update calibration language.** "What did they decide?" beats "How polished was the artifact?"
- **04 — Build AI-fluency signal into interviews.** Not a tools test — a judgment test. *"Tell me about a time you overrode an AI suggestion. Why?"*
- **05 — Partner earlier on org design.** Don't be the team handed the new shape. Be the team that helps draw it.

**Speaker note:** All five are doable in the next 8–12 weeks. Pick one and commit publicly inside HR. The recruiting one (02) is the highest-leverage — if hiring managers are calibrated wrong, every other downstream HR motion compounds the error.

### Slide 21 — Amplify, in one slide
Small label: **AMPLIFY**
Title: *The Payoneer education for every Payoneer employee.*

Three cards:
- **What it is.** A company-wide learning program designed for the AI era — practical, role-relevant, continuous.
- **Why it matters.** "Go learn AI in your own time" doesn't scale. We need shared fluency, shared vocabulary, shared standards.
- **Why HR is central.** Adoption is a culture problem before it's a curriculum problem. That's your turf.

**Speaker note:** Tailor specifics based on the actual Amplify launch plan — dates, format, audiences. Keep the language consistent with the official launch comms.

### Slide 22 — HR's role in the AI shift ⭐ REFRAMED
Small label: **YOUR PART — BEYOND AMPLIFY**
Title: *What we need from HR in the AI shift.*

Three cards (was four — removed "Model it"):

- **Champion the shift.** You're the trusted voice for the people who are most nervous about AI. Don't outsource the reassurance. The narrative they hear from you matters more than the one they read in the all-hands deck.
- **Surface the signal.** You'll hear who's adopting, who's resisting, who's quietly anxious, where managers are stuck. That signal goes back into how the company designs the rollout — Amplify, Foundry, and everything around them.
- **Connect it to careers.** Make AI fluency visible in growth conversations, JDs, calibration. Not as a threat — as the opportunity it actually is.

**Speaker note:** Amplify is the most visible vehicle, but the AI shift is bigger than any one program. Frame HR's role as ongoing, not project-bound.

# SECTION 5 — LEAVE WITH THIS (5 min)

### Slide 23 — Leave with this (mirrors PM deck slide 19)
Small label: **LEAVE WITH THIS**
Title: *Four things to keep. One thing to do.*

Four things:
- **Stay closer to people, not further.** When work compresses, relationships are the differentiator.
- **Coach for judgment, not output.** That's the new evaluation lens — for you and for the people you support.
- **Update the artifacts that describe work.** JDs, rubrics, ladders, performance language. Most are stale.
- **Treat the AI shift like a posture, not a project.** Amplify is a vehicle; the shift is the road.

**One thing to do this week:**
> Pick one HR artifact you own — a JD, a rubric, an interview guide. Rewrite it through an AI-era lens. Bring it to your next 1:1.
