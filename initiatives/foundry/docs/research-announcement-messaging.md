# Research: AI Academy All-Hands Announcement — Messaging & Best Practices

## 1. Messaging Frameworks from Major Corporate AI Academies

### Real Company Examples

**Amazon ("AI Ready" initiative, Nov 2023)**
Amazon committed to providing free AI skills training to 2 million people by 2025. Internal messaging centered on "AI is a once-in-a-generation opportunity, and we want everyone to be ready." Key framing: AI literacy as a *career asset*, not a corporate mandate. The internal rollout used the language of readiness — "be ready for what's next" — which positions the company as enabling, not demanding.

**JPMorgan Chase (2024)**
JPMorgan mandated AI training for all new asset & wealth management employees and rolled out tools to 200,000+ employees. CEO Jamie Dimon framed it publicly: "AI is not going to eliminate jobs — but people who use AI will eliminate the jobs of people who don't." Internally, messaging centered on *competitive advantage* — "our clients deserve advisors who use every tool available." The key insight: they tied AI skill to *professional identity*, not compliance.

**PwC ("My AI" program, 2023-2024)**
PwC invested $1B in AI upskilling across all 75,000 US employees. Their messaging framework: "AI is a tool that makes good consultants great." They avoided framing it as "learn AI or fall behind" and instead used "AI amplifies what you already do well." The program name "My AI" was deliberately personal — it's *your* AI, not the company's AI. Key insight: **possessive framing** makes it feel like a gift, not an assignment.

**Google (internal AI training, 2023-2024)**
Google's internal messaging (per reports from employees) used the framing "we built these tools — we should be the best in the world at using them." For a company that makes AI, the angle was pride/ownership. For non-AI companies, the adaptable version is: "The best companies don't just adopt AI — they become AI-fluent."

**Microsoft (AI learning paths, 2024)**
Microsoft structured internal AI upskilling around "copilot" language — AI as your assistant, not your replacement. The messaging avoided technical jargon and instead used workflow language: "Imagine your Monday morning standup takes 10 minutes instead of 45." Concrete, relatable scenarios > abstract promises.

**Accenture (2024)**
Invested $2B in AI, trained 250,000+ employees. Messaging: "Every role at Accenture is an AI role." This is the boldest version of inclusive framing — no carve-outs, no "for technical staff only." They paired this with a clear tier system so people could enter at their level.

### Messaging Pattern Synthesis

The most effective corporate AI education messaging follows this structure:

| Element | Pattern | Example |
|---------|---------|---------|
| **Anchor** | Start with the world changing, not the company changing | "AI isn't coming — it's here" |
| **Bridge** | Connect the change to individual relevance | "And it changes what 'great at your job' looks like" |
| **Reframe** | Position learning as power, not obligation | "This is your chance to get ahead of it" |
| **Proof** | Show it's already working somewhere | "Team X cut their review cycle from 5 days to 5 hours" |
| **Invitation** | Make participation feel like joining something, not being assigned something | "We built this for you. First cohort starts April." |

## 2. "What's In It For Me" Framing

### The Three Audiences in Every All-Hands

When announcing an AI program, you're speaking to three groups simultaneously:

1. **Enthusiasts (15-20%)** — Already using AI, want structure and permission to go further. They need: "We're making this official and giving you resources."
2. **Curious middle (50-60%)** — Interested but haven't invested time. They need: "Here's the easy on-ramp, and it's worth your time."
3. **Skeptics/anxious (20-30%)** — Fear ranges from "this is hype" to "this replaces me." They need: "This makes you more valuable, not less."

### Proven WIIFM Messaging Patterns

**The "10x Your Best Work" Frame**
Don't say: "Learn to use AI tools"
Say: "Your best work, done in a fraction of the time"

This works because it starts with *their* work quality, not a tool. People care about being good at their jobs. AI is the accelerant.

**The "Career Insurance" Frame**
Don't say: "AI skills are required for the future"
Say: "A year from now, AI fluency will be as expected as Excel fluency. Get ahead of that curve now, on company time, with support."

This works because it acknowledges the stakes without threatening. "On company time" is crucial — it signals investment in them.

**The "Unfair Advantage" Frame**
Don't say: "We need to be more productive"
Say: "Imagine walking into your next meeting having already analyzed three weeks of data, drafted three scenarios, and pressure-tested each one. That's what AI-fluent PMs do."

This works because it paints a *specific, vivid scenario* the audience recognizes from their own work.

**The "Already Happening" Frame**
Don't say: "We're rolling out AI training"
Say: "Some of your colleagues are already using AI to do in 30 minutes what used to take a full day. We want everyone to have that advantage."

This leverages social proof + FOMO without naming and shaming anyone.

### Overcoming Fear and Resistance

**The key insight from organizational psychology:** Fear of AI isn't about the technology — it's about status threat. People worry about looking incompetent, becoming irrelevant, or being exposed as less skilled than they appear.

**Effective counter-messaging:**

| Fear | Counter-Message |
|------|----------------|
| "AI will replace me" | "AI replaces tasks, not people. The people who use AI replace the people who don't." (JPMorgan framing) |
| "I'm not technical enough" | "If you can write an email, you can use AI. That's literally how it works — you type what you need in plain language." |
| "This is just hype" | "Whether or not AI changes everything, people who can use it are already outperforming those who can't. The ROI is real today." |
| "I'll look stupid learning" | "Everyone starts at zero. The program is designed for exactly that." |
| "I don't have time" | "The first thing AI gives you back is time. The ROI kicks in from Session 1." |

## 3. Bottom-Up Change Messaging

### The Empowerment Frame vs. The Mandate Frame

**Mandate frame (avoid):**
"The company is rolling out an AI transformation. All employees are expected to..."

**Empowerment frame (use):**
"We're giving you the tools and training to work differently. You decide how to apply it to your world."

### Key Language Patterns

**"You, not we"** — The subject of every sentence should be the employee, not the company.
- Not: "We are investing in AI training"
- Instead: "You're getting access to an AI academy"

**"Built for you, by people like you"** — When session leads are peers (PMs teaching PMs, engineers teaching engineers), emphasize this. It signals bottom-up, peer-driven learning.
- "This isn't consultants lecturing you. Your colleagues — people who do your job — designed every session around real work."

**"Opt-in energy, even if broadly available"** — The psychology of choice matters enormously. Even if you want everyone to participate, the framing should feel like invitation.
- "Spots are limited for the first cohort" (scarcity + exclusivity)
- "Apply to join" rather than "You've been enrolled"

**"Individual agency" language:**
- "You choose your path"
- "Work at your pace"
- "Apply to your own workflows"
- "Build your own toolkit"
- "Your AI, your way" (PwC's model)

### The "Movement" Frame

The most powerful bottom-up messaging positions the program as a *movement* people join, not a program they attend:

- "The Foundry isn't a training program — it's a community of people who are changing how work gets done at Payoneer."
- "This started because a few of us saw what was possible. Now we're opening it up."
- "Every person who goes through this becomes part of something bigger."

This works especially well for Payoneer's two-pillar model (Academy + Community) — the Community pillar literally IS the movement.

## 4. "No One Left Behind" — Inclusive Framing

### Companies That Did This Well

**Accenture's "Every Role Is an AI Role"**
The boldest inclusive framing. No exceptions, no qualifiers. When they said every role, they meant HR, finance, legal, marketing, engineering — all of it. The key: they backed it with a tiered learning system so non-technical roles had an appropriate entry point.

**PwC's "My AI" — Personal, Not Technical**
By making the program name personal ("My AI"), PwC signaled that this was about individual empowerment, not technical skill. The entry-level content focused on email drafting, meeting prep, and document review — universal tasks.

**Amazon's "AI Ready" — Readiness, Not Expertise**
The word "ready" is deliberately accessible. You don't need to be an expert — you need to be ready. Low bar, high invitation.

### Inclusive Messaging Patterns

**For non-technical audiences specifically:**

| Message | Why It Works |
|---------|-------------|
| "If your job involves writing, reading, analyzing, or communicating — AI is for you." | Describes everyone's job without naming any role |
| "This isn't coding. This is thinking with a powerful new tool." | Directly addresses the #1 misconception |
| "The biggest AI gains we've seen aren't in engineering — they're in operations, finance, and customer-facing roles." | Flips the assumption |
| "You don't need to understand how AI works. You need to understand how to make it work *for you*." | Separates use from understanding |

**The Tier System as Inclusion Signal:**
When you show a progression (Makers -> Builders -> Masters), it implicitly says: "There's a level for you, wherever you are." The existence of Level 1 says "beginners welcome" more powerfully than any words.

**The Foundry-Specific Angle:**
With Academy + Community, the messaging writes itself:
- Academy = deep training for those ready to commit
- Community = broad access for everyone, no prerequisites
- "Everyone has a place in The Foundry. The Academy is for deep dives. The Community is for everyone who wants to explore, learn, and share."

## 5. Announcement Deck Structure (4-5 Slides)

### The Narrative Arc

The best internal launch decks follow a three-act structure compressed into 4-5 slides:

**Act 1: The World Changed (1 slide)**
**Act 2: Here's What We're Doing About It (2 slides)**
**Act 3: Here's How You Join (1-2 slides)**

### Slide-by-Slide Blueprint

**Slide 1: "The Shift" (Context)**
- Purpose: Create shared urgency without fear
- Content: 2-3 data points showing AI's impact on your industry/function
- Tone: "This is happening. It's exciting. And it changes things."
- Visual: Simple, bold. One stat or one image.
- Example data points for Payoneer context:
  - "X% of financial services firms have active AI programs" (McKinsey data: 72% of organizations have adopted AI as of 2024)
  - "Teams using AI report 30-50% time savings on routine analysis" (various industry surveys)
  - A concrete internal example if you have one: "Last month, [team/person] used AI to [specific result]"

**Slide 2: "Our Response — The Foundry" (The Reveal)**
- Purpose: Introduce the program with pride and ambition
- Content: Program name, logo/brand, one-line description, the two pillars
- Tone: "We didn't just buy a tool. We built something."
- This is where brand identity matters — a named program with its own visual identity signals seriousness
- Key line: "The Foundry is Payoneer's AI academy and community — built by our people, for our people."

**Slide 3: "How It Works" (Structure)**
- Purpose: Make the program tangible and navigable
- Content: The tier system, both tracks, the Community pillar
- Visual: A simple progression diagram (Makers -> Builders -> Masters) + the Community alongside
- Key insight: People need to *see themselves* in the structure. Each tier should have a one-line "this is for you if..." descriptor.
- Don't overload — this slide should feel simple and inviting, not like a course catalog.

**Slide 4: "What You'll Walk Away With" (Outcomes)**
- Purpose: Make it concrete and aspirational
- Content: 3-4 specific outcomes per level
- This is the WIIFM slide — the payoff
- Frame as capabilities, not knowledge: "You'll be able to..." not "You'll learn about..."
- Examples:
  - "Draft a complete PRD in 20 minutes instead of 2 hours"
  - "Analyze a quarter's worth of customer data in a single conversation"
  - "Build a working internal app — no engineering required"

**Slide 5: "Join The Foundry" (CTA)**
- Purpose: Convert interest to action
- Content: How to sign up, timeline, first cohort details, QR code/link
- Tone: Invitation + urgency
- Include: "First cohort: [date]. Limited to [N] spots."
- The "one more thing" element can go HERE — a surprise: a celebrity instructor, a hackathon prize, executive participation, a credential/badge, or "everyone who completes the program gets X."

### The "One More Thing" Technique

Apple's reveal technique works in corporate settings when deployed correctly:

**Setup:** The main announcement feels complete. You've shown the program, the structure, the timeline. The audience thinks they have the full picture.

**The pivot:** "And one more thing..."

**The reveal options for The Foundry:**
- **Executive commitment:** "Every member of the senior leadership team will go through The Foundry alongside you." (Signals this isn't delegated down — leaders believe in it.)
- **External recognition:** "Graduates earn a credential recognized by [external body/university]."
- **The Community reveal:** If you present Academy first as the "main course," the Community pillar becomes the "one more thing" — "But we didn't want to limit this to cohorts. So we built something for everyone..."
- **An early win story:** "We actually piloted this with 5 people last month. Here's what happened..." followed by a concrete result.
- **The alumni commitment:** "Every Foundry graduate becomes a mentor for the next cohort. This isn't a one-time program — it's a permanent part of how Payoneer works."

**Key rule:** The "one more thing" should feel *generous* — it's a gift to the audience, not another demand. It should make people say "oh, wow" not "oh, great, more work."

## 6. Call-to-Action Best Practices

### What Makes People Actually Sign Up

Research on internal program enrollment consistently shows these drivers:

**1. Scarcity + Exclusivity**
- "First cohort limited to 20 per track" — immediately creates urgency
- "Applications open for 2 weeks" — deadline-driven action
- "Selected based on [criteria]" — positions participation as an achievement
- Accenture and Google both used cohort caps for initial AI training to create demand that exceeded supply

**2. Social Proof**
- Name-drop early adopters or pilot participants (with permission)
- "Your colleagues [Name], [Name], and [Name] are leading sessions" — seeing peers as faculty is powerful
- "X people have already signed up" — real-time counter if possible
- Show a quote from someone who's already experienced it

**3. Low Perceived Barrier**
- "90 minutes per session" — the smallest unit sounds manageable
- "No prerequisites for Level 1" — explicitly say this
- "You'll be productive with AI by the end of Session 1" — fast payoff promise
- "On company time" — no personal sacrifice required

**4. Visible Leadership Endorsement**
- The most powerful enrollment driver in corporate settings is *who announces it*
- If the CEO/CPO announces or endorses, enrollment rates jump 2-3x vs. HR/L&D announcements
- Having Oren or Yaron say even one sentence of endorsement in the all-hands has outsized impact
- Even better: "I'm signing up too."

**5. Concrete, Immediate Value Promise**
- "After Session 1, you'll know which AI tool to use for which task"
- "After the first week, you'll have cut at least one recurring task in half"
- "You'll build a working AI workflow in your first session"
- People don't sign up for "AI literacy" — they sign up for specific outcomes

**6. Remove Friction**
- One-click signup (not a multi-step application)
- QR code on the announcement slide
- Slack channel to join immediately (community belonging starts before the program)
- Calendar holds sent automatically upon registration

### The Registration Funnel

Best-in-class internal programs use a two-step funnel:

1. **Interest capture** (all-hands moment): "Scan this QR code to save your spot" or "Join #the-foundry on Slack" — zero-friction, immediate. This captures the momentum from the announcement.

2. **Formal enrollment** (within 48 hours): Follow up with a short form — role, track preference, level self-assessment. This is where you filter and assign cohorts.

The mistake most companies make: they go straight to step 2 at the all-hands, which kills momentum. Capture interest first, formalize second.

### The 48-Hour Rule

Internal program announcements have a 48-hour window where enthusiasm is highest. After that, it drops precipitously. Your CTA strategy should account for this:

- **During the all-hands:** Low-friction interest capture (QR code, Slack channel)
- **Same day, after all-hands:** Follow-up email with program details + registration link
- **Day 2:** Social proof message — "X people have already registered"
- **End of week:** Manager endorsement — direct managers encouraging their teams

## 7. Messaging Recommendations Specific to The Foundry

Based on the above research, here are tailored recommendations:

### The Foundry's Unique Strengths to Emphasize

1. **Built internally, by peers** — This isn't off-the-shelf training. PMs teach PMs. Engineers teach engineers. The content is designed around Payoneer's actual work, with real examples from your codebase, your data, your customers.

2. **Two pillars = something for everyone** — Academy for deep commitment, Community for broad access. This IS "no one left behind" made structural.

3. **Named program with identity** — "The Foundry" is a strong name. It evokes creation, craft, transformation. Use it as a brand, not just a label.

4. **Clear progression** — Makers -> Builders -> Masters gives people a visible path. "I'm a Maker" becomes an identity, not just a status.

### Headline Options

- "The Foundry: Where Payoneer Learns to Build with AI"
- "AI isn't the future. It's Tuesday. The Foundry gets you ready."
- "The Foundry: Built by us. For us. Starting now."
- "Your AI journey starts here. The Foundry — Payoneer's AI Academy."

### One-Liner Pitch Options

- "A structured, hands-on program where you learn to use AI the way it should be used — applied to your actual work, with real results from Session 1."
- "The Foundry is where Payoneer people become AI-fluent — not through lectures, but by doing real work with AI tools, guided by colleagues who've already figured it out."
