## Part I: Organizational Psychology — What Team Science Knows About Micro-Teams

### 1. Optimal Team Size

**Hackman & Vidmar (1970)** — Groups of 2-7 at Yale. The optimal size was **4.6 members** (the crossing point where "too small" and "too large" ratings intersect). Paradoxically, mid-sized groups (3-5) were *less creative* than dyads or groups of 6-7.

**Hackman (2002), *Leading Teams*** — Team *design* is approximately **40x as powerful as coaching** in affecting performance. Hackman never allowed teams larger than 6 in his Harvard classes. His lifelong conclusion: **4-6 members** for complex interdependent work.

**Belbin** — Argues the optimum is **4 people**, with a critical threshold:
- **3-person teams**: Personalities have *greater impact* on decision-making; the team is *more vulnerable* — even small changes may affect cohesion.
- **4-person teams**: The team has "a life of its own" — membership may change without threatening integrity. This threshold does *not* exist at 3.
- With 9 Belbin roles and only 3-4 slots, each member must play 2-3 roles — which works because "most of us have more than one Team Role strength."

### 2. Process Loss and Its Inversion

**Steiner (1972)** — Actual productivity = potential productivity - process losses (coordination loss + motivation loss). At 3-4 members, process losses are near-minimal because the full communication graph is cognitively manageable (3-6 links).

**Mueller (2012)** — 212 knowledge workers across 26 teams (3-19 members). Identified **"relational loss"** as a missing element: employees in larger teams perceived less support. In micro-teams, each person maintains full relational support with every other member. *N=212, published in Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes.*

**Ringelmann Effect** — Per-person output drops ~50% going from 1 to 8 members (rope-pulling, 1913). But the effect is minimal in groups of 2-4, where individual contributions remain identifiable. Pronounced only above 5-6.

### 3. Social Loafing: Eliminated — But Replaced

**Karau & Williams (1993)** — Meta-analysis of **78 studies**. Social loafing is "robust and generalizes across tasks and subject populations." But it is **completely eliminated** when the task is personally involving, contributions are unique, and outputs are identifiable. All three conditions are structurally true in a 3-person team.

**The Kohler Effect (Kerr et al., 2007)** — Opposite of social loafing. Weaker members work *harder* in groups than alone, driven by upward social comparison and indispensability. "The more indispensable individuals perceive their efforts to be, the greater effort they exert." At 3-4 people, everyone's contribution is indispensable — predicting systematic motivation *gains*.

**The flip side: role overload** — When loafing disappears, it may be replaced by chronic overload. 47% of technologists report being forced to take on tasks outside their job functions. Role overload triggers psychological strain that *undermines performance* — potentially offsetting motivation gains. **Micro-teams create a paradox: motivation is high but capacity is constrained.** *(Peng et al., 2021, Frontiers in Psychology)*

### 4. Triad Dynamics (Simmel, 1908)

**Core insight:** The shift from 2 to 3 is *qualitatively different* from any subsequent addition. It introduces a "supra-individual character" absent in dyads.

**Key dynamics:**
- **Tertius gaudens** ("the rejoicing third") — a third party benefits from competition between the other two
- **Tertius iungens** ("the uniting third") — a third party mediates and bridges
- **Structural instability** — Triads inherently tend to decompose into a coalition of two plus an outsider. "There can be no group of three in which, at one point or another, the third isn't seen as an intruder."

**Caplow (1968)** — Extended Simmel with formal predictions. Counterintuitive: "strength can be a weakness" — the strongest party may end up dominated because the two weaker members rationally ally against them. Directly relevant to teams with a nominal "lead."

### 5. Triads vs. Quads: Decision-Making

**Scientific Reports (2021)** — Triads outperform tetrads because majority rule provides a natural conflict-resolution heuristic. Tetrads suffer "learning incoherence" from persistent 2-2 splits. **3 is better than 4 for decision speed.**

**But:** Majority rule in a triad means one person is *always* outvoted. A repeated pattern of the same person in the minority creates resentment.

**LePine et al. (1997)** — In 51 four-person teams, when the weakest member lacked cognitive ability, others *helped*. When they lacked *conscientiousness*, they were *ignored*. Micro-teams develop nuanced deference based on the *type* of weakness.

### 6. Psychological Safety at Micro-Scale

**Edmondson (1999)** — Foundational: psychological safety is a *team climate* property, not interpersonal trust. Associated with learning behavior, which mediates the relationship to performance. *51 teams, published in ASQ.*

**Google Project Aristotle (2015)** — 180 teams. Psychological safety was the **#1 factor** in effectiveness. "Who is on a team matters less than how the team members interact."

**Small team paradox:**
- **Advantage**: With only 3-6 bilateral relationships, risk assessment for speaking up is simpler. "It's an easier mental calculation in a group of 3 versus a group of 30."
- **Risk**: No anonymity, no dilution. One toxic relationship represents 33% of all relationships. If safety fails with even one person, the damage is proportionally enormous.

**Dunbar's layers** — A 3-4 person team sits within the **5-person support clique** — the layer of highest trust and most intense relational investment. This is *both* the enabler of deep safety (trust is possible) and the amplifier of its failure (conflicts hit at a deeply personal level).

### 7. Communication and Information Sharing

**Metcalfe's Law at small N:** 3 people = 3 links. 4 = 6. 5 = 10. 10 = 45. 20 = 190. Going from 3 to 4 doubles links, but both numbers are cognitively manageable. The explosion becomes problematic above ~7-8.

**Hidden Profile Research (Lu, Yuan & McLeod, 2012)** — Meta-analysis of 65 studies. Groups mentioned **two standard deviations more** shared information than unique information. Larger teams focused *more* on shared information, leading to higher failure rates in detecting hidden profiles. Micro-teams are better at surfacing unique knowledge, but the risk isn't eliminated.

**Groupthink (Janis, 1972)** — Key conditions: high cohesion, structural faults, external stress. A highly intimate 3-4 person team is *precisely* the profile. But a single dissenter represents 25-33% of the group, making dissent harder to ignore than in groups of 20.

### 8. Team Cognition: Transactive Memory

**Wegner (1985, 1987)** — Originally studied in intimate couples. A transactive memory system (TMS) is "who knows what" — individuals specialize and rely on others as external memory.

**Small team advantage:** TMS forms *faster* in small groups — continuous face-to-face communication, complete mental models of each person's expertise, and a directory small enough for working memory.

**Small team fragility:** Ellis et al. (2005) found that teams with stronger TMS performed better after member loss, *but* the benefit was reduced when a *critical member* left. In a 3-person team, losing one member means losing 33% of the TMS directory *and* all the knowledge that person held. **TMS is maximally fragile at minimum team size.**

### 9. Diversity at Minimum Scale

**The inverted U-shape** — Multiple studies show cognitive diversity has a **curvilinear relationship** with performance. Moderate diversity maximizes group problem-solving. *(Aggarwal & Woolley, 2019, Frontiers in Psychology)*

**Woolley et al. (2010)** — 699 people in groups of 2-5, published in *Science*. A general "collective intelligence" factor explains 30-40% of variance. Primary contributors: social sensitivity, equality of conversational turn-taking, and individual general intelligence. **At 3-4 people, individual member characteristics dominate. One member low in social sensitivity can crater collective intelligence.**

**The 3-4 slot constraint:** You cannot optimize for all diversity dimensions simultaneously. Research suggests what works in a 10-person team may not scale down — at 3-4 people, individual ability may dominate over diversity effects. *(Hong & Page, 2004)*

### 10. Small Teams and Innovation Type

**Wu, Wang & Evans (2019)** — Analysis of **65 million papers, patents, and software products** spanning 1954-2014, published in *Nature*. Small teams tend to *disrupt* with new ideas; large teams tend to *develop* existing ones. Small teams cited older, less popular ideas (deeper search), while large teams cited recent, highly-cited work. **The strongest quantitative evidence (N = 65M) that team size predicts innovation type.**

## Part II: HR & Management Practice — How Organizations Have Run Micro-Teams

### 1. Military Fire Teams (Established, 80+ years)

**Navy SEAL fire teams** — 4-person units with specialized roles (team leader, medic/sniper, comms, sonar). The design principle: every person is irreplaceable because every person has a unique skill, forcing true interdependence.

**Special Forces ODA (A-Teams)** — 12 people with deliberate redundancy: two NCOs in each of five specialties. The redundancy model (two of each) is a direct answer to the bus factor. Enables "split team" 6+6 operations.

**Fighter pilot wingman pairs** — The smallest military unit. The wingman's job: protect the lead, "at the cost of giving up an easy kill." At the 2-person scale, trust isn't optional — it's the entire operating model. (Oswald Boelcke, 1915)

### 2. Other Historical Precedents

**Lockheed Skunk Works (1943)** — Kelly Johnson built America's first jet fighter with 23 engineers and 30 mechanics in 143 days. Rule #3: "The number of people must be restricted in an almost vicious manner. Use a small number of good people (10-25% of the normal number)." Everyone understood the full picture, decisions happened through conversation, and poor performers had nowhere to hide.

**Surgical teams** — Small, role-specialized units with extreme interdependence. Key finding: **dedicated operating teams** (same group working together over time) show measurable improvements in mortality rates, turnover time, teamwork, communication, and costs vs. ad-hoc assembly.

### 3. Company Examples

**37signals/Basecamp (20+ years, established)** — Teams of **2** (one designer + one programmer). "If it takes more than two people, it can't. They'll simplify it down, make more tradeoffs." They deliberately downsized from 3-person to 2-person teams. Extreme scope discipline is the enabling condition.

**Amazon Two-Pizza Teams (evolved away)** — Designed to reduce coordination overhead. **Key lesson: team size wasn't the bottleneck — inter-team dependencies were.** Amazon evolved to Single-Threaded Leadership: a leader dedicated 100% to one initiative. Few people inside Amazon talk about two-pizza teams anymore.

**Valve's Cabal Model (mixed results)** — Mini-teams of one of each discipline, no managers. Worked for small creative bursts. **Failed at scale:** "Months of discussion without leadership resulted in no action." The model couldn't produce content fast enough for Half-Life 2.

**Buurtzorg (15+ years, established)** — 15,000+ nurses in self-managing teams of max 12. No managers. 40% fewer care hours than industry average, 8% overhead vs. 25%, highest patient satisfaction in the Netherlands. Validated by KPMG and E&Y. **Works because of clear professional norms (nursing) and measurable outcomes.**

**Haier / RenDanHeYi (12+ years, radical)** — Fired 12,000 middle managers, split into 4,000+ microenterprises with their own P&L, hiring, and strategy. Works but requires fundamental cultural transformation most organizations can't execute.

**Zappos Holacracy (failed)** — 14-18% staff left. 150 departments became 500 circles. Complexity multiplied. Business metrics suffered. Quietly abandoned.

**Spotify Squads (aspirational, never fully implemented)** — The widely-cited model was "only ever aspirational" per Jeremiah Lee's 2020 critique. The useful contribution: **Chapters and Guilds** for career development outside the squad.

### 4. Hiring for Micro-Teams

**Google Project Aristotle** — "Who is on a team matters less than how they interact." But in micro-teams, who is on the team *determines* how they interact — there's no crowd to dilute a misfit.

**Sutton (Stanford, 2007) — "The No Asshole Rule"** — Even one toxic person can poison a culture. At small scale, the impact is catastrophic. IDEO's practice: offer jobs only to candidates who've interned and "demonstrated under real working conditions that they aren't assholes."

**Morgeson et al. (2005)** — Social skills, conscientiousness, and teamwork knowledge predict contextual performance in teams *beyond* task-specific skills. But there is "relatively little empirical research on how to select individuals specifically for team-based settings."

### 5. Career Pathing Without Ladders

**The core problem (Gallagher):** Without hierarchical grades, career pathways become opaque. Employees leave to feel they are advancing.

**Gallup (2025):** Manager engagement is lowest with 1-2 direct reports, peaks at 8-9. A manager of a 3-4 person team is in an engagement trough — stuck between IC and "real" manager. 97% of managers have IC responsibilities, spending a median 40% on IC work.

**T-shaped professionals (Hansen & von Oetinger, HBR 2001):** Freely share ideas across the company (horizontal bar) while committed to business unit performance (vertical bar). Particularly relevant for flat organizations where lateral growth replaces upward promotion.

**Guild model (Spotify Chapters):** Separates "who you work with on the product" (squad) from "who develops your career" (chapter). Chapter Leads serve as both working squad members and people managers for career development. Useful pattern for micro-teams that can't provide internal ladders.

### 6. Knowledge Management and Bus Factor

In a 3-person team, every person represents **33% of knowledge and capability**. The bus factor is literally 1.

**Pair programming meta-analysis (Hannay et al., 2009):** Faster for low-complexity tasks, higher quality for high-complexity tasks. **Regular pair rotation** prevents knowledge silos and increases bus factor.

**Special Forces ODA model:** Two of each specialty. The military's direct answer to the bus factor in small teams.

### 7. Team Lifecycle and Longevity

**Straube et al. (2020)** — Meta-analysis, 622 effect sizes across 169 studies. Team tenure is positively related to performance. But the relationship is **curvilinear**: R&D teams show a U-shape — high creativity when new, dipping in moderate tenure (familiarity-breeds-contempt phase), recovering with longer tenure.

**IMD (2025):** Executive surveys show core team tenure has shrunk from 1.5-2 years to about **1 year** — team composition changes every 12 months through additions or departures.

### 8. Self-Managing Teams

**Laloux (2014)** — "Teal" organizations built on self-management, wholeness, and evolutionary purpose. Three structural patterns: parallel teams, web of contracts, and nested teams.

**HBR (2016) on Holacracy:** "Most companies shouldn't adopt these principles wholesale — a piecemeal approach usually makes sense."

**Hackman's key insight:** Leaders have greatest leverage *before* work begins — by designing the team and its context — not by monitoring continuously.

## Part III: Employee Wellbeing & Mental Health in Micro-Teams

### 1. Isolation and Loneliness

**Holt-Lunstad et al. (2015)** — Landmark meta-analysis: social isolation increases mortality risk by **29%** and loneliness by **26%** — comparable to smoking 15 cigarettes a day.

**Dunbar's layers create a structural problem:** The 15-person "sympathy group" is the layer that provides emotional support and daily social context. A 3-person team falls below even the 5-person intimate support group. A micro-team of 3 **structurally cannot fulfill the social belonging needs that humans require from their work community.**

**Workplace loneliness cascades (Hickey et al., 2023, *Occupational Medicine*):** Predicts poorer supervisor-rated performance, weaker organizational commitment, reduced engagement, greater dissatisfaction, and increased turnover. A 2025 prospective study confirmed loneliness directly predicts job turnover within 6 months.

**Remote work amplifies it:** Employees working remotely 3-4 days/week have significantly higher odds of loneliness. For a 3-person remote team, isolation compounds — neither in-team critical mass for social fulfillment nor office environment for serendipitous connection.

### 2. Role Overload and Burnout

**The paradox:** Role overload initially drives high performance and learning ("challenge stressor"). But sustained overload without recovery depletes resources and leads to burnout. Early overload feels stimulating; the trap is that it doesn't self-correct.

**Karasek's Demand-Control Model (1979):** High demands + high autonomy = "active" jobs with motivation and learning. But in a 3-person team, "control" is illusory — you have autonomy in theory but no ability to say no. The real experience: **high demand + constrained capacity = strain.**

**The autonomy paradox:** 87% of employees are engaged with autonomy, but 50% feel overwhelmed by it. Leaders giving unconditional job autonomy actually *increase* stress, nervousness, and overwhelm.

**Startup burnout as proxy:** CEREVITY 2025 survey of 127 tech founders: 73% experience "shadow burnout" (persistent exhaustion hidden behind performance). 65% of startup failures stem from founder burnout or internal conflict, not market conditions.

**Maslach's six areas of work life** — All six are strained in micro-teams simultaneously: work overload (inevitable), lack of control (no delegation), insufficient rewards (no progression), breakdown of community (too few people), absence of fairness (workload equity visible and contentious), value conflicts (wearing hats you didn't sign up for).

### 3. Absence and Fragility

**In a 3-person team, one absence removes 33% of capacity.** This isn't arithmetic — it's structural fragility.

**Presenteeism research (CIPD):** 26% of employees feel guilty about colleagues picking up their work if they call in sick. 36% believe no one can cover for them. In a micro-team, these aren't perceptions — they are facts. People work sick, underperform, make errors, and potentially infect teammates.

**Conservation of Resources Theory (Hobfoll, 1989):** When resources are lost (teammate out), remaining resources cover the gap, depleting further, making the team more vulnerable to the next absence. **"Loss begets loss"** — the spiral is faster and more devastating when the resource pool is tiny.

**Hartwig et al. (2020):** A change in team configuration due to members leaving/joining leads to temporal disruption. For a 3-person team, any single change is a **33% configuration shift** — not a ripple but an earthquake.

### 4. Interpersonal Conflict at Micro-Scale

**Simmel (1908):** Structural exclusion is inherent to triads. At any moment, only one pair can interact, forcing the third into the margins. Coalition formation is not pathological — it is structural.

**No escape:** In larger teams, conflict between two people can be managed through avoidance, reassignment, or buffer roles. In a triad, there is nowhere to go. Person C is either drawn in as mediator (adding emotional labor to role overload) or forced to pick sides (creating a 2-vs-1 coalition).

**The spectator dynamic:** When strong tension exists between two members, the remaining member disengages. In a larger team, spectators are a minority. In a triad, **one spectator = one-third of the team disengaged.**

### 5. Cognitive Load and Decision Fatigue

**Context switching costs 40% of productive time.** Recovery time between tasks: 9-23 minutes. In a micro-team where each person covers multiple functions, context switching isn't occasional — it is the job.

**Attention residue (Leroy, 2009):** After switching from task A to task B, thoughts about A persist for up to 20 minutes. In a triad shifting from code review to customer call to strategic planning, you are never fully present for any task.

**Decision fatigue (Baumeister):** Sustained decision-making depletes executive function. In larger teams, decisions are distributed. In a triad, every person participates in every decision — no delegation buffer.

### 6. Psychological Safety: The Fishbowl Effect

**The visibility-vulnerability paradox:** In a 3-person team, everything is visible. You cannot have a quiet bad day. This creates competing dynamics:
1. Intimacy can accelerate trust-building (you truly know each other)
2. The fishbowl effect can suppress vulnerability (no anonymity, no crowd to hide in)

**The politeness-transparency paradox:** In teams where maintaining harmony is prioritized, psychological safety can paradoxically lead to lack of accountability. In a triad where you depend entirely on two colleagues, the cost of honest conflict feels existentially high.

### 7. Identity and Belonging

**Baumeister & Leary (1995):** Humans need "a minimum quantity of lasting, positive, and significant interpersonal relationships." The need requires both frequent positive interactions AND stable bonds.

**Social Identity Theory (Tajfel & Turner, 1979):** People derive professional identity from group membership. In a micro-team with fluid roles, professional identity becomes unstable — "Am I a PM? A designer? A QA engineer?" Identity anchoring weakens.

**The belonging gap is structural:** Even a high-functioning 3-person team cannot provide the social diversity, mentorship hierarchy, peer benchmarking, and professional community that a larger team offers. **External community is not a nice-to-have; it is a psychological necessity.**

### 8. AI-Specific Cognitive Fatigue

**BCG/HBR "AI Brain Fry" study (March 2026)** — 1,488 U.S. workers:
- **14%** of AI users report acute cognitive fatigue
- **3-tool sweet spot**: productivity peaks at 3 simultaneous AI tools; beyond 4, productivity plummets
- High AI oversight demands → 14% more mental effort, 12% more fatigue, 19% more information overload
- Those affected → 33% more decision fatigue, 39% more major errors, 34% intend to quit
- Workers describe "buzzing sensation," mental fog, difficulty focusing, headaches

**For micro-teams this compounds:** Each person in a 3-person AI-native team oversees multiple AI agents across multiple domains, hitting the 4+ tool threshold quickly. No one to hand oversight to when fatigued.

**Ju & Aral (2025, MIT)** — Field experiment with 2,234 participants:
- Human-AI teams sent 45% more messages total, but **23% fewer social messages**
- **84% fewer direct edits** to AI output (delegation over refinement)
- 50% more output per worker, but reduced interpersonal warmth
- Humor, concern, apologies, and rapport-building all decreased

**Implication:** In a 3-person AI-native team where much collaboration is human-AI, the already-thin interpersonal connection gets thinner. The social communication that builds cohesion is systematically suppressed by the work pattern itself.

## Part IV: Synthesis — Key Tensions for 3-4 Person Teams

| Dimension | Advantage | Risk |
|-----------|-----------|------|
| Process loss | Near-zero coordination loss; full graph connectivity | No slack — every absence is catastrophic |
| Social loafing | Eliminated (identifiability + indispensability) | Replaced by role overload and burnout |
| Psychological safety | Simpler risk calculus; deeper trust possible | One toxic relationship = 33% of all relationships |
| Communication | Complete information sharing; no lossy channels | Groupthink risk from high cohesion |
| Decision-making | Fast (triads have majority rule) | Coalition dynamics (2-vs-1); deadlock in quads |
| Team cognition | TMS forms fast; complete knowledge of who-knows-what | TMS is maximally fragile; bus factor = 1-2 |
| Diversity | Each hire has outsized impact on composition | Only 3-4 slots; can't optimize all dimensions |
| Innovation type | Structurally favors disruption over development | May lack breadth for complex multi-domain execution |
| Belonging | Deep intimacy possible within the support clique | Below Dunbar's minimum; can't fulfill social needs |
| Career | High autonomy, broad skill development | No ladder, no progression, identity instability |
| AI collaboration | Massive productivity gain per person | Cognitive fatigue, suppressed social communication |

## Part V: Protective Factors — What the Research Recommends

These are evidence-based interventions specifically applicable to micro-teams:

1. **Mandatory external community** — Guilds, communities of practice, or peer groups outside the team. Addresses belonging gap and Dunbar deficit. *(Cohen & Wills, 1985; Smite et al., 2019 on Spotify guilds)*

2. **External mentorship** — Each team member paired with a mentor outside the team. Addresses identity, career pathing, and emotional support that the team structurally can't provide. *(SHRM: 57% higher engagement with mentoring programs)*

3. **Structured rotation or pairing** — Periodic work with other teams to prevent insularity. *(Pair programming meta-analysis: regular rotation increases bus factor and knowledge sharing)*

4. **Explicit coverage protocols** — Documented cross-training so absence doesn't mean guilt. The Special Forces model: two of each specialty. *(Hobfoll's COR theory: prevents loss spirals)*

5. **Proactive workload monitoring** — Regular capacity assessments, not just project tracking. The 3-tool guideline for AI usage. *(BCG 2026: productivity plummets beyond 4 tools)*

6. **Conflict resolution training for triads** — Standard approaches assume larger groups. Triad dynamics (coalition, exclusion, mediation) require specific training. *(Simmel, 1908; Caplow, 1968)*

7. **Deliberate social ritual** — Shared non-work activities, since the team is too small for organic social emergence. *(Hakanpaa et al., 2022: task interdependence alone insufficient)*

8. **Hiring for chemistry, not just capability** — Personality fit, social sensitivity, and interpersonal compatibility are structural requirements, not nice-to-haves. *(Woolley et al., 2010: social sensitivity is the top contributor to collective intelligence)*

9. **Career pathways outside the pod** — T-shaped growth, guild leadership, cross-pod rotation, scope expansion. The pod cannot be the career container. *(Hansen & von Oetinger, HBR 2001; Spotify Chapters model)*

10. **Cognitive load management** — Oversight rotation for AI review tasks, deliberate recovery periods, 3-tool ceiling. *(BCG 2026; Leroy 2009 on attention residue)*

## Appendix: Evidence Quality Summary

### Well-Established (meta-analyses, large N, replicated)
- Social loafing elimination in small teams (Karau & Williams, 78 studies)
- Optimal team size ~4.6 (Hackman & Vidmar, replicated conceptually)
- Small teams disrupt, large teams develop (Wu et al., N = 65M, *Nature*)
- Hidden profile bias increases with group size (Lu et al., 65 studies)
- TMS predicts performance and forms faster with co-training (multiple replications)
- Collective intelligence depends on social sensitivity (Woolley et al., replicated, *Science*)
- Social isolation mortality risk (Holt-Lunstad, meta-analysis)
- Team tenure positively relates to performance (Straube, 169 studies)

### Moderately Established (consistent findings, fewer replications)
- Triads outperform tetrads on learning coherence (single study, aligns with theory)
- Relational loss mediates size-performance (Mueller, N=212)
- Kohler effect in conjunctive tasks (replicated in lab, limited field evidence)
- Belbin's threshold at 4 (practitioner-derived, not experimentally isolated)
- Workplace loneliness predicts turnover (prospective study, 2025)
- AI cognitive fatigue at 4+ tools (BCG, N=1,488, single study)

### Theoretical/Analytical (strong logic, limited direct evidence)
- Psychological safety dynamics at micro-scale specifically (no direct studies of 3-4 person teams)
- Groupthink vulnerability at micro-scale (Janis studied small groups but not micro-teams)
- Dunbar's layer mapping to team design (correlational framework, not causal)
- Autonomy paradox in micro-teams (inferred from general autonomy research)
- AI suppression of social communication in micro-teams (Ju & Aral studied pairs, not triads)
