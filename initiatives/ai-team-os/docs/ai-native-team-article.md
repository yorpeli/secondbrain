
THE AI-NATIVE
PRODUCT TEAM

Smaller teams. Faster execution. Higher bar - and what it takes to make it work




Yonatan Orpeli
VP of Product
March 2026
 
Executive Summary
AI is compressing the distance between understanding a problem and shipping a solution. The artifacts and processes that filled that gap - PRDs, handoff specs, estimation sessions, alignment meetings - were never the work. They were bridges. When the distance shrinks, the bridges become unnecessary, and teams can be smaller, faster, and fundamentally closer.
This document proposes a team model built for that reality: a core pod of three - one Product Manager, two Engineers - augmented by AI agents and supported by shared organizational functions. This is not a scaled-down version of today’s teams. It’s a different structure entirely, designed around what becomes possible when capable people work without the coordination overhead that larger teams were built to manage.
The argument is organized in four parts:
How the team works. A new development loop - from continuous discovery through co-solving to ship - that replaces the sequential handoff model. A blast radius governance framework that defines the team’s operating space: what they can do freely, what requires consultation, what needs approval.
Who’s in the team. The PM who prototypes, analyzes, and curates context, whose primary value is product sense and judgment about what to build and why. Two engineers focused on system design, integrity, and orchestrating AI agents rather than writing every line of code.
The human side. What it actually feels like to work in a trio. Why psychological safety becomes structural rather than cultural. Why loneliness is a design problem. Why one person leaving is existential, not disruptive. And what organizations must do about it.
The enterprise lens. How regulated and complex organizations calibrate this model - tighter governance thresholds, compliance as an architectural input, and why the discipline that enterprises require makes AI-native teams better, not slower.
We have already begun. The PM job description is written and in active recruitment. The Engineer role is being co-developed with Engineering leadership. The next step is standing up the first pod - a real team, with a real problem, operating as described here.
This is an opinion. An informed one, but an opinion. The proof will come from practice.

The Shift
The most important thing AI changes about product teams isn’t speed. It’s distance.
And this has been coming for a long time. Every major methodology shift in software development has been a compression of the same gap. Waterfall measured it in quarters. Agile shortened it to sprints - but preserved the fundamental structure: PM specifies, engineer builds, handoff between them. What AI changes is not the length of the loop but the loop itself. When building costs approach zero, the separation between deciding what to build and building it stops making economic sense.
In traditional organizations, the distance between the person who understands the problem and the person who builds the solution is large. A product manager synthesizes customer needs into a document, hands it to design, waits for mockups, passes those to engineering, waits for questions, clarifies, waits for a build, reviews it, finds mismatches with the original intent, sends it back. Each step is a translation. Each translation loses fidelity.
That distance creates an entire ecosystem of artifacts and processes: product requirement documents, design handoff specs, detailed tickets, multi-step approval flows, status meetings, alignment sessions. These aren’t bureaucratic overhead - they are bridges. They exist because the people who need to collaborate can’t simply work together directly. The more people involved, the more bridges required. The more bridges, the slower the crossing.
AI compresses that distance. A product manager can prototype a working product in an afternoon. An engineer can explore the problem space without waiting for a spec. Two people can direct an AI agent to build what they’re discussing and iterate in real time - changing direction mid-sentence, testing alternatives in minutes instead of sprint cycles. This may also change the scope of what a team works on - smaller, more focused problem spaces become viable when the cost of a dedicated team drops.
When distance shrinks, the bridges become unnecessary. And when the bridges go away, the team can be smaller, faster, and fundamentally closer to the problem. What doesn’t change is the environment: the market, the users, the regulatory cycles, the pace of customer adoption. Teams move faster, but the world they operate in doesn’t speed up with them. That constraint is important - it means faster execution must be paired with sharper judgment about what’s worth building, not just more output.
But speed without direction is just faster failure. When execution was expensive and slow, bad product decisions took months to manifest - and the process itself created friction points where misalignment could surface. A spec review, an estimation session, a design review - each was an opportunity for someone to say “wait, is this actually the right problem?” When a team can go from idea to working product in a day, a wrong assumption becomes a shipped mistake by Friday. And this collaboration increasingly happens in shared environments - including the IDE itself, where PMs fluent enough in AI tools can work alongside engineers, directing agents and iterating on design components in the same workspace.
Building the wrong thing perfectly is the new risk. This is why the PM’s product sense - their continuous discovery, their customer understanding, their ability to frame the right problem - becomes the team’s most critical asset. Speed amplifies whatever you point it at, good or bad. Strong product judgment makes a fast team dangerous to competitors. Weak product judgment makes it dangerous to itself.
This is not a story about replacing people with AI. It’s a story about what becomes possible when a small group of capable people works without the friction that large teams were designed to manage - and what’s required of the people who remain.

How the Team Works
The Old Loop
The traditional product development cycle was built around handoffs: PM researches → PM writes spec → Design creates mockups → Engineering estimates → Engineering builds → QA tests → PM validates → Ship.
Each arrow is a handoff. Each handoff introduces delay, context loss, and misinterpretation. The spec captured the PM’s understanding at the moment of writing - but understanding evolves. By the time engineering starts building, the PM has learned more. The spec is already stale.
This process existed because it had to. Specialists worked in sequence because the cost of keeping everyone engaged simultaneously was too high. The result was a system optimized for coordination rather than creation.
The New Loop
In an AI-native team, the loop collapses into five continuous phases: Continuous Discovery → Problem Frame → Co-Solve → Harden → Ship + Measure - feeding back into Continuous Discovery.
Continuous Discovery
This is the foundation - not a phase that happens before building, but an ongoing discipline. The PM maintains deep understanding of customers, the market, competitive dynamics, and the problem space. They talk to users. They watch how the product is used. They track what competitors are doing. They understand the business context.
This is the work that makes the PM a PM. Not everything the PM learns will end up in production. That’s the point. The PM knows the broader landscape so that when the team sits down to build, their choices are informed by rich context - not just the immediate request.
In an AI-native team, discovery is also accelerated. AI can synthesize customer feedback at scale, surface patterns in usage data, monitor competitive signals, and draft research summaries. The PM spends less time gathering and more time interpreting.
Problem Frame
A short, sharp articulation of the opportunity - not a PRD. What’s the problem, in concrete human terms? For whom? Why now? How will we know we’ve solved it? What constraints exist?
One page, not ten. The problem frame is an invitation to collaborate, not a contract to fulfill. It gives the engineer enough context to contribute meaningfully to the solution - without prescribing the solution.
The key difference from a traditional spec: the problem frame doesn’t say what to build. It says what problem to solve. The “what to build” emerges from co-solving, where the PM and engineer figure it out together.
Co-Solve
This is where the real shift happens. The PM and engineer work together - not in sequence, but simultaneously - to converge on a solution.
The PM brings the problem context, user understanding, and business judgment - they validate that the solution creates real value and serves the right user need. The engineer brings system knowledge, technical feasibility, and an instinct for what will be robust - they validate that the solution can actually be built, scaled, and maintained. AI does the mechanical work of actually building: generating code, creating interfaces, wiring up integrations. The humans direct, evaluate, and decide.
In practice: the PM describes what the user needs. The engineer thinks about what the system supports. They direct an AI agent to build a first version. It appears in minutes. The PM says “the language is wrong for this market.” The engineer says “this approach won’t scale when we add more document types.” They adjust, the agent rebuilds, and within an hour they have a working product that reflects both product judgment and technical reality.
This is not a throwaway mock-up. Modern AI tools generate production-quality code. The output may need hardening, but it’s a real, functional product - ready for customer input and feedback, not just a picture of one.
A PM who wants to test three different approaches no longer needs three sets of requirements, three estimates, and three sprint commitments. The PM and engineer prototype all three in a day, test them with real users, and decide based on evidence rather than speculation. The quality of decisions goes up because the cost of exploration goes down.
This matters precisely because of the speed argument. The faster the team moves, the more dangerous it is to build in the wrong direction. Co-solving is the structural answer: misalignment surfaces in the first hour, not after the first sprint. The PM sees the product taking shape and can course-correct in real time. The engineer raises feasibility concerns before they become architectural debt. The most expensive failure mode in traditional product development - building the wrong thing and discovering it weeks later - is eliminated not by slowing down, but by making direction-setting and building the same activity.
Harden
Engineer-led: architecture, reliability, edge cases, security, and compliance get addressed. The engineer examines AI-generated code with a critical eye. Does the architecture support future changes? Are error cases handled? Is the data model right? Are there security vulnerabilities?
This is where engineering depth matters most. AI can generate code quickly, but it doesn’t inherently understand the production context - specific performance requirements, compliance constraints, integration points with existing systems. The engineer ensures that what ships is not just functional but trustworthy.
The PM stays involved - not as a gate, but as context. When the engineer asks “should we handle this edge case?” the PM is right there with the answer. No context has been lost.
Ship + Measure
The PM watches how users respond. The engineer watches how the system performs. What they learn feeds back into discovery - which has been running the whole time.
The cycle time - from problem frame to shipped product - can be days or weeks instead of months. Not because corners are cut, but because the waste is gone.
The Operating Space
Autonomy without boundaries isn’t autonomy - it’s ambiguity. When a team doesn’t know what decisions it can make independently, one of two things happens: people guess conservatively and slow themselves down, or someone makes a call they shouldn’t have, and the organization clamps down on everyone.
The pod model requires a clear answer to the question: what can the team do without asking? We use a blast radius framework - five levels that calibrate independent authority to potential impact:
Level	Authority	Example
L0 - Self-serve	Team acts and documents. No sign-off needed.	UI copy change, internal tool, dashboard update
L1 - Inform	Team moves, then notifies stakeholders.	New tracking, minor flow optimization, non-regulated feature
L2 - Consult	Team aligns with partner before acting.	New data collection, customer-facing flow changes, vendor evaluation
L3 - Approve	Explicit approval required.	Changes to KYC logic, new vendor integration, pricing changes
L4 - Strategic Gate	Leadership alignment required.	New country rollout, regulatory commitment, new product line

These levels apply equally to human decisions and to what AI agents are permitted to do autonomously. An AI agent might generate code freely and modify non-regulated features at L0–L1, but require human review for any change touching compliance logic at L2 and above. The framework governs the output, regardless of whether a human or an AI produced it.
The vast majority of daily decisions are L0–L1. The team moves fast by default, with governance escalation only where stakes warrant it. This isn’t governance imposed on the team - it’s the mechanism that grants autonomy. Without clear boundaries, organizations default to “ask permission for everything” because the cost of a mistake is undefined. The framework makes the cost explicit, so the team can move freely within it.
The specific calibration of levels depends on context - what the team works on, who the customer is, what the regulatory environment requires. An internal tools team has a different blast radius profile than a payments team. Both need the framework. The enterprise-specific calibration is discussed later.

Who’s in the Team
The Core Pod: Three People
The AI-native product team is three people. This is not arbitrary - it reflects a deliberate design choice about what produces the best outcomes when AI augments human capability.
Three is the largest number where everyone can work together without coordination overhead. At four, you start needing subgroups and synchronization. At two, you lack redundancy. Three people can have a single conversation, share a single context, and make decisions without meetings about meetings.
The industry is converging on this range. Reforge shipped five AI products in nine months with four-to-five person teams. LinkedIn replaced its Associate Product Manager program with “Full-Stack Builder” pods. Andrew Ng has described experiments where the PM-to-engineer ratio flips entirely - two PMs per one engineer - because AI makes execution cheap while product judgment becomes the bottleneck.
Our model: one PM, two Engineers.
1 Product Manager
The PM owns the problem space. They are the team’s connection to customers, to the market, and to the business context that determines what’s worth building.
What expands. AI gives the PM capabilities that previously required separate specialists:
Prototyping. The PM builds a working demonstration of an idea in hours, not weeks. This isn’t about the PM becoming an engineer. It’s about the PM being able to show what they mean instead of only describing it.
Data analysis. The PM pulls usage data, runs funnel analyses, interprets A/B tests, and monitors metrics directly. The PM who once asked an analyst “how is this performing?” and waited days now gets the answer in minutes.
Research synthesis. The PM processes large volumes of customer feedback, competitive intelligence, and market data. What once required weeks of manual analysis can be synthesized in hours.
Context curation. The PM maintains the living context that feeds the team and its AI agents: problem frames, customer understanding, business constraints. This means designing the information environment in which the team operates.
What contracts. No 10-page PRDs. No detailed acceptance criteria that try to anticipate every question. No lengthy estimation sessions. That conversation happens in real time, during co-solving.
What remains essential - and what this exposes
The PM’s primary value is product sense and judgment about what to build and why. This judgment comes from continuous discovery - deep customer understanding, market awareness, strategic thinking, and the ability to say “no” to work that doesn’t matter.
Here’s the uncomfortable truth: AI didn’t raise the bar for product managers. The bar was always this high. AI just removed the hiding places.
In the old model, a PM could spend weeks on a well-crafted PRD, run a thorough estimation session, manage a backlog meticulously, and look productive - without ever forming an independent view on whether the team was solving the right problem. The process was the output. The artifacts - the PRD, the roadmap deck, the status update - were visible and measurable. The thinking behind them was not.
When AI compresses execution, those artifacts lose their value. What remains is the judgment: Do you understand the customer well enough to frame the right problem? Can you make the tradeoff between what users want and what the business needs? Do you know when to push forward and when to kill a direction? Can you say “we’re building the wrong thing” when the evidence points that way, even if the team has momentum?
These capabilities were always what separated great PMs from average ones. The difference is that in an AI-native team, there’s nowhere to hide behind process. The PM either has the judgment or they don’t - and it shows immediately, because the team moves fast enough that bad decisions surface in days, not quarters.
This means the talent bar for PMs in this model is genuinely high. Not because AI added new requirements, but because it stripped away the work that previously allowed weaker PMs to appear functional. The PM on an AI-native team must be someone who can demonstrate ideas - prototype, analyze data, and curate context - not just describe them. More importantly, they must have the product sense and instinct to direct a team that can build anything in a fraction of the time it used to take.
The PM doesn’t grow within product management as it was traditionally practiced. They grow into it - developing the judgment, customer intuition, and strategic sense that the role has always demanded but rarely enforced.
2 Engineers
The engineers own the system. They are responsible for the architecture, reliability, security, and technical integrity of what the team builds.
The shift. The traditional engineer’s day was dominated by writing code - translating requirements into implementation, line by line. In an AI-native team, AI agents handle much of the code generation. The engineer’s focus shifts from writing code to directing, reviewing, and orchestrating it.
This requires a different kind of depth. Not the ability to type code fast, but the ability to recognize when code is right, subtly wrong, or fundamentally flawed. Reviewing AI-generated code demands strong architecture sense, security awareness, and an instinct for edge cases.
When code generation is cheap, the valuable engineering work moves upstream: system design, API boundaries, data models, performance architecture, integration strategy. The engineer thinks at the system level, not the function level. The traditional frontend/backend split weakens. What matters more than layer specialization is understanding of the system as a whole - how data flows, where failures propagate, what scales and what doesn’t.
The engineer increasingly directs multiple AI agents working in parallel - one generating tests, another refactoring a module, a third scaffolding a new feature. The skill is in orchestration: setting context, defining boundaries, and integrating results into a coherent system.
Why two, not one. One engineer can’t cover both the breadth of a modern product surface and the depth required for reliability and security. Two engineers create natural pairing for code review, redundancy for knowledge continuity, enough capacity for both feature work and system maintenance, and a peer relationship that supports growth.
The specific engineering profiles will vary by team context. A product-facing team might want two full-stack engineers. A platform team might pair a systems architect with a reliability engineer. The “two engineers” flex to match the work.
Shared Functions
Not every capability belongs inside the pod. Some skills require deep specialization that can’t be maintained in a 3-person team. Some work benefits from consistency across the product surface. The principle: the application of each skill gets absorbed into the pod, while the deep expertise and infrastructure sits in shared teams.
Design. The pod handles day-to-day design decisions directly. The PM sketches interfaces during co-solving. Engineers build with design system components that enforce visual consistency. Shared designers provide the design system itself, complex UX challenges that span multiple pods, visual identity and brand coherence, and design reviews that maintain quality standards. As AI makes rough output cheap, the demand for design quality increases. The shared design function raises the bar.
Platform Engineering. The pod deploys its own work - this is a requirement for speed. Platform engineering makes self-service deployment safe and efficient: automated pipelines, monitoring, security tooling, infrastructure as code. The platform absorbs the complexity so pods can focus on product.
Data Infrastructure. The PM handles most analytical work directly - AI tools make this accessible. The shared data function shifts from analytics service to data infrastructure: quality, pipeline reliability, data modeling, and access governance. The data team doesn’t analyze data for the pod - it ensures the pod can analyze data for itself.
Security / Compliance. In any enterprise context, compliance and security aren’t optional. The shared function provides expert guidance, security reviews, audit support, and guardrails. The relationship is partnership, not gatekeeping - the pod consults compliance during the problem frame, not after the build.
Team Variations
The pod described above is the default, the “vanilla” team - a product-facing team serving external users. In practice, the model flexes on four dimensions: who the customer is (external users vs. internal engineers vs. operations teams), the blast radius ceiling (how much governance the domain requires), what kind of engineering dominates (full-stack product vs. ML vs. infrastructure), and how much domain expertise is required (general vs. deep regulatory knowledge). These dimensions change the team's profile - engineer specializations, PM depth, relationship with shared compliance - but not the structure itself. Autonomy driven by context and principles. Same loop. Different calibration.
The Human Contract
A 3-person team is not a scaled-down version of an 8-person team. It’s a fundamentally different social structure. The dynamics are closer to a founding team than to a traditional squad.
Most writing about AI-native teams focuses on structure, roles, and tooling - and skips over what it actually feels like to work this way. The human dimension is not a soft add-on to the operating model. It is the operating model. Three people who trust each other, communicate well, and share ownership will outperform any org chart. Three people who don’t will fail regardless of how elegant the structure looks on paper.
This is harder to diagram than a team topology. It doesn’t lend itself to frameworks and acronyms. But it’s the part that determines whether this works or doesn’t.
Psychological Safety at Micro-Scale
In an 8-person team, one difficult relationship is manageable. The team absorbs it, other relationships compensate, and the impact is diluted. You can work around someone. You can minimize contact.
In a 3-person team, one difficult relationship is fatal. There is nowhere to hide, no buffer, no one to route around. Every interaction involves the same three people. A disagreement that festers doesn’t just affect two people - it poisons the team. A person who withholds feedback, reacts defensively, or avoids conflict doesn’t just slow one relationship - they break the team’s ability to function.
This compounds in regulated environments. When your work carries real consequences - regulatory penalties, wrongly denied service, financial exposure - the psychological cost of unresolved tension is amplified. A trio that can’t have a direct conversation about a compliance concern because someone is defensive is a trio that ships risk.
This means hiring for collaborative chemistry is not a soft criterion - it is a structural requirement. A brilliant engineer who cannot work in a tight trio is a worse hire than a good engineer who can. The interview process must evaluate not just skill but the ability to give direct feedback without aggression, receive criticism without defensiveness, disagree openly and resolve disagreements without lingering resentment, and maintain composure under pressure.
This is not a nice-to-have. It is the single biggest predictor of whether a pod succeeds or fails.
Loneliness Is a Design Problem
Small teams can be isolating. When your entire professional world is two other people, you lose things that larger groups provide naturally.
Ambient learning. In a larger team, you overhear conversations about problems you’re not working on, pick up techniques from colleagues, develop a broader understanding of the product. In a trio, your learning is limited to what your two teammates share.
Social variety. Human beings need diverse social interaction. The same three people, every day, for months, can feel confining - even when the relationships are healthy.
Professional identity. Engineers identify with other engineers. PMs identify with other PMs. In a 3-person pod, the discipline community disappears unless the organization actively provides it.
Support networks. When work is hard, people turn to trusted colleagues outside their immediate team. In a pod, there is no “outside the team” unless it’s built deliberately.
This isn’t sentimental. Isolation affects creativity (fewer inputs lead to narrower thinking), retention (people leave when they feel disconnected), and resilience (no support network when a teammate leaves). The organization must strengthen community beyond the pod - reinforcing guilds, building cross-pod rituals, investing in discipline communities. It will not emerge naturally from a structure this small.
Fragility and Knowledge Loss
If one person leaves an 8-person team, the team absorbs it. Seven people retain the context, the relationships, the momentum.
If one person leaves a 3-person team, you’ve lost a third of your capability and a third of your context. If the PM leaves, the team loses its connection to customers, the strategic direction, and the accumulated understanding of why things were built the way they were. If a strong engineer leaves, the team loses architectural knowledge, system understanding, and half its review capacity.
This is the fundamental fragility of small teams, and it must be addressed explicitly.
Documentation is the primary mitigation. In a 3-person team, knowledge that exists only in someone’s head is a single point of failure. The team must maintain living context: architecture decisions and their rationale, problem context and customer insights, technical choices and the tradeoffs they represent. Not in a separate wiki that drifts out of sync, but in the repository and working documents the team uses every day.
The good news: AI agents can generate and maintain most of this documentation. After every significant decision, after every architectural change - an agent captures what happened and why. The discipline isn’t in the writing. It’s in ensuring it happens consistently.
Cross-pod connections matter too. Guild participation, shared context, and cross-pod collaboration mean that when someone leaves, there are people in the broader organization with some familiarity with the work. Not a replacement, but a bridge.
Shared Ownership Without Territorial Ambiguity
“That’s not my job” can survive in a large team. There’s enough specialization that boundaries make sense. In a trio, it’s fatal. If the PM notices a bug and doesn’t flag it, it ships. If the engineer sees a UX problem and ignores it, users suffer.
Everyone owns the outcome. But pretending roles don’t exist is equally naive. The PM and engineers bring genuinely different skills and perspectives. The PM sees the user’s frustration where the engineer sees the system’s constraint. These perspectives are complementary, not interchangeable - and the tension between them is productive.
The contract must be explicit: we all own the outcome, and we each bring distinct strengths to it.
When disagreements arise - and they will, frequently, in a team moving this fast - there needs to be clarity about who has the call. The PM owns problem-space decisions: what to build, for whom, what success looks like. The engineer owns technical-integrity decisions: how to build it, what architecture to use, what tradeoffs to accept on performance and reliability. Everything in between - scope, priority, UX tradeoffs, build-vs-buy - they co-own and resolve through discussion.
If a pod is escalating frequently, the chemistry is wrong. That’s a signal, not a process problem.
Growth Beyond the Pod
“Where do I go from here?” is a real question when your team is three people. You can’t promote someone into a role that doesn’t exist. You can’t grow by managing a team that’s already at its intended size.
Career growth in a pod-based organization comes from different sources: scope expansion (the pod takes on a larger problem space), cross-pod movement (gaining breadth across the product), guild leadership (setting standards, mentoring across pods), and cross-cutting roles (architecture review, platform strategy that spans multiple pods).
The organization must provide these paths explicitly. They won’t emerge naturally.
This is also where the talent pipeline risk becomes real. If we only staff pods with experienced people and provide no entry-level pipeline, we’ll face a leadership and burnout crisis within a few years. AI adoption is already reducing junior hiring across the industry. The growth paths described above - mentoring, guild participation, cross-pod collaboration - must deliberately create space for developing the next generation.

What the Organization Must Provide
Small, autonomous pods are powerful - but only if the organization around them provides the right foundation. The pod model is not a way to shrink the organization. It’s a way to restructure it: less coordination overhead, more shared infrastructure.
A Documenting Organization
When teams are small and knowledge is concentrated in few people, documentation becomes a survival mechanism. Not more documents - a different relationship to documentation.
The traditional approach: documentation is something you create when asked, usually after the fact, usually reluctantly. It’s an output, not a capability.
The AI-native approach: documentation is continuous and embedded. It exists because the team needs it - to operate effectively, to recover from disruption, and to enable AI agents to contribute.
The key insight: documentation that helps a new human team member understand the system is exactly the documentation that helps an AI agent work within it. The agent configuration file that tells an AI assistant how the codebase is organized also helps a new engineer navigate it. The architecture decision record that explains why a pattern was chosen also helps AI avoid patterns that were considered and rejected.
AI agents can generate and maintain most of this documentation. The organizational discipline isn’t in the writing - it’s in making documentation a natural, embedded part of the workflow.
Concrete mechanisms: agent configuration files at the root of every repository, architecture decision records maintained alongside code, git hooks enforcing doc-code sync, periodic auto-commits by development agents creating natural restore points, and knowledge vaults - structured collections of decisions, patterns, and learnings.
A Learning Organization
Without active sharing, pods become islands. Each develops its own tools, patterns, and vocabulary. What one pod learns stays locked inside that trio. The organization doesn’t learn - individual teams do.
Cross-pollination must happen at two levels:
Individual level. Engineers need a community of engineers. PMs need a community of PMs. Guilds and communities of practice where people learn from peers, share techniques, and maintain professional identity beyond their trio. This directly addresses the loneliness problem.
Team level. Pods sharing what they’ve learned: tools that worked, workflows that failed, patterns that solved common problems, mistakes that others should avoid. When one pod discovers that a particular approach dramatically improves outcomes, every pod should know. When one pod makes a mistake, every pod should learn from it without repeating it.
Infrastructure Prerequisites
Repository readiness. Clean, well-structured codebases with good test coverage and consistent conventions. AI coding tools are dramatically more effective in well-organized repositories. Legacy spaghetti code gets worse with AI assistance - the AI generates more code that follows the spaghetti pattern, accelerating tech debt.
Context infrastructure. Shared documentation, architectural decisions, coding conventions, and agent configuration files that enable both humans and AI to work effectively. Every major system should have context files that describe it.
Tooling standardization. Standardized AI development tools, access to AI models, and shared patterns for common workflows. When every pod uses the same tools and conventions, cross-pollination becomes natural.
Governance and compliance framework. Clear policies on what data AI can access, what code AI can generate in regulated systems, what requires human review, and how AI-assisted decisions are audited.
Measurement baseline. Before restructuring, the organization must know its current velocity, quality metrics, cycle times, and cost-to-serve. Without a baseline, it’s impossible to know whether the new model is actually better.

The Enterprise Lens
The blast radius framework introduced earlier applies to every team. But in large, regulated organizations - financial services, healthcare, enterprise SaaS - the calibration is different, because the consequences of mistakes are real and specific.
A compliance error in identity verification can result in regulatory penalties. A bug in eligibility logic can wrongly deny service to real people who depend on the product to receive payments or operate their businesses. A security vulnerability in a payment flow can expose financial data.
These aren’t abstract risks. They’re the operating environment. This doesn’t make AI-native teams impossible in the enterprise. It makes them different - and ultimately better.
How Enterprises Calibrate the Framework
In a regulated enterprise context, the blast radius levels shift. Changes that might be L0 in a consumer app - self-serve, no sign-off - may be L2 or L3 when they touch regulated flows. The thresholds are tighter at the upper levels. More decisions require consultation or explicit approval. The shared compliance function becomes almost a virtual fourth team member for pods working in regulated domains.
But the framework still enables speed. The vast majority of daily work - internal tooling, non-regulated features, analytics, prototyping - remains L0–L1. The team moves fast on everything that doesn’t carry regulatory risk, and applies deliberate governance only where the stakes require it. The discipline is in the calibration, not in blanket slowness.
Compliance as a Capability
In a traditional team, compliance review is a gate at the end - the team builds, then submits for review, then gets questions, then reworks. Compliance feels like friction because it’s applied too late.
In an AI-native team, compliance is woven in from the start. The PM’s problem frame includes regulatory constraints as part of the design space. The engineer builds with compliance as an architectural input - the data model, audit trail, and access controls are design decisions, not bolted-on features. AI agents operate within defined guardrails enforced at the infrastructure level. The shared compliance function is a partner available for early consultation, not just end-of-process review.
The enterprise variant of the AI-native team isn’t a compromised version of the startup model. It’s a more mature version - one where autonomy is earned through trust, governance, and demonstrated judgment. The discipline that regulated enterprises require is exactly the kind of deliberate, well-governed operating model that makes AI-augmented teams trustworthy at scale.

Where We Are and What Comes Next
This document describes a target state. We are not there yet. Being honest about the gap is as important as being clear about the destination.
Current State
Today, our product teams are structured in the traditional model. PMs write specs. Engineers build to those specs. Handoffs, estimation sessions, and sequential workflows are the norm. Team sizes are larger. Coordination overhead is real. Our existing DLC process reflects exactly this pattern - structured phase gates and handoffs between functions.
Some individuals are already working differently. PMs are prototyping with AI tools. Engineers are using AI agents for code generation and review. But these are individual practices, not team-level operating models. The infrastructure - repository readiness, context documentation, governance frameworks - is not yet built for the pod model.
The talent profile is also mixed. Some PMs and engineers would thrive in a pod structure immediately. Others would need development. And some - this is the honest part - are better suited to the traditional model. AI didn’t raise the bar. It removed the hiding places. That’s a real transition with real human implications, and how we handle it matters.
What We’ve Started
This is not a white paper. It’s the first phase of something already in motion.
The roles are defined and in active recruitment. The job descriptions - built from this framework - is with iTalent, our recruitment partner, and is being used to hire a Principal PM and specific engineering profile. The PM profile reflects everything described here: strong product sense, AI fluency, and the ability to thrive in a small, high-trust team.
Role are still being co-developed. This is deliberate – We’re working with the AI talent G2G track and feeding on each other work to make sure they get the team aspect and we get their input on hiring process and team structures.
What Happens Next
Stand up one pod. Give it a real problem, the infrastructure to operate as described, and people who want to work this way - volunteers, not conscripts. Ensure access to shared functions: design, platform, data, compliance. Define the blast radius calibration for their specific domain.
Adapt our hiring process. Finding real talent will be hard, our process will need to change to make sure it does not create unnecessary friction for candidates, but keeps our adapted needs and focus areas front and center to make sure we hire the right talent – professionally, and personally 
Learn from the first pod. What works? What breaks? Where does the model need to flex? What infrastructure was missing? What surprised us about the human dynamics? How does co-solving actually feel when it’s three real people, not a model on paper?
Then decide what’s next. Scale what works. Fix what doesn’t. Acknowledge that the model will evolve as AI capabilities evolve - what we design today will look different in twelve months. That’s not a weakness; it’s the nature of building during a period of rapid technological change.
The goal is not to arrive at the perfect team structure. The goal is to build the organizational capability to work this way - and to learn faster than the technology changes underneath us. This model is designed to be composable: as it matures, other teams and initiatives - including efforts like G2G and AI-DLC that should adapt and support the work of these teams.


This is an opinion. An informed one - shaped by research, external conversations with leaders at companies already working this way, and our own experience building with AI daily. But the proof will come from practice, not from documents about practice.
